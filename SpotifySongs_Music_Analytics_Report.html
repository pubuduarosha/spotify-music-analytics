# Creating box plots of 'speechiness' for each genre
plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))
plt.xticks(rotation=45)  # Rotate x-axis labels for better readability

plt.xlabel('Genre')
plt.ylabel('Speechiness')
plt.boxplot([df[df['playlist_genre'] == genre]['speechiness'] for genre in df['playlist_genre'].unique()],
            labels=df['playlist_genre'].unique(), patch_artist=True)
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
The described plot is a collection of box plots, each representing the distribution of 'speechiness' within a different musical genre. The 'speechiness' feature measures the presence of spoken words in a track, with higher values indicating more speech-like sounds and lower values indicating more music-like sounds. Here's a narrative description of what the plot might reveal: - Each box plot would show the median, quartiles, and potential outliers for 'speechiness' within a genre. - Genres with a higher median 'speechiness' might include rap or hip-hop, where spoken word is a key component of the music. - Genres like instrumental or classical might have very low 'speechiness' values, as they typically contain little to no vocal content. - The range of 'speechiness' within a genre could indicate its diversity in vocal content. A genre with a wide box and far-reaching whiskers might have a mix of instrumental tracks and tracks with spoken words. - Outliers in the plot could represent tracks that are atypical for their genre in terms of 'speechiness', such as a spoken word track in a genre that is typically instrumental. - Comparing the box plots side by side would allow us to see which genres are more speech-centric and which are more music-centric. - The plot could also reveal if there are any genres that have a particularly wide range of 'speechiness', suggesting a diversity of musical styles within that genre. Overall, the box plots would provide a visual comparison of 'speechiness' across genres, telling a story about the role of vocals and spoken words in different types of music.

The described plot would be a series of Kernel Density Estimate (KDE) plots, each representing the distribution of tempos within a specific genre. The x-axis would show the tempo in beats per minute (BPM), and the y-axis would represent the density of tracks at each tempo value. Each genre would have its own KDE curve, potentially with different colors to distinguish them.

In this plot, we might observe that certain genres have peaks at different tempos, indicating the most common tempo range for that genre. For example, EDM might show a peak at higher BPMs, reflecting the genre's energetic and danceable nature. In contrast, a genre like jazz might have a broader distribution with a peak at lower BPMs, suggesting a more relaxed tempo.

The plot would allow for comparisons between genres. If one genre's curve is wider, it suggests a greater variety of tempos within that genre. A taller, narrower peak would indicate a strong preference for a specific tempo range within that genre.

The use of fill under the KDE curves would make the plot visually appealing and easier to interpret the density of the distribution. The title "Tempo Distribution by Genre" would clearly convey the plot's focus, and the labeled axes would ensure that the viewer understands what is being measured.

Overall, the plot would tell a story about the rhythmic characteristics of different genres, offering insights into how tempo contributes to the identity and feel of each musical style.
